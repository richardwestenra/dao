Hey, I’m Richard. I’m a front-end developer. I want to talk to you about how sometimes good is better than perfect.

Has anybody here read A Dao of Web Design?

It’s an article in A List Apart by John Allsop, and it’s really great, definitely check it out.

It was written way back in the year 2000,

2000

This was a bit before my time, so if I’ve got anything wrong, let me know.

Basically, the year 2000 was the Dark Ages of web development

The First Browser War was just winding down, IE 5 was crushing Netscape 4.5,
and people were still arguing about whether this newfangled CSS thing would take off.

At the time, there was a culture war going on between print and web designers.

Print Designers

versus

Web Designers

Most designers at the time came from a print design background, and they were used to having complete control over how their designs would look.

The web is a flexible medium

But the web is a really flexible medium. When you design for the web, you’ve got to take into account that people will be viewing your web page on loads of different computers, and it’ll look completely different from one computer to another.

So when you’re designing, you have to take account of different screen sizes and resolutions, different colours, brightness and contrast, different system fonts and default font-sizes, and different browsers with different rendering engines, each with their own idiosyncratic interpretations of web standards.

The Chaos is a strength

But what John Allsop argued in A Dao of Web Design is that this chaos isn’t a weakness of the web: The chaos is a strength.

The flexibility and ubiquity of the web aren’t problems, they’re possibilities. They make the web more accessible, and allow it to be used in ways that we could not have imagined fifteen years ago.

New tools

New problems

On the other hand, we now have dozens of browsers and hundreds of versions running on thousands of devices.

We’ve all got smartphones in our pockets, and tablets, and phablets, and smart watches, and you can even browse the web on your glasses if you don't mind looking like a tool.

New tools?

Perfection
is hard

So it’s still really hard to make things perfect.

We now have great power, but we still don't have complete control.

The Law of Diminishing Returns

One problem is that we end up fighting with the law of diminishing returns.

This is a principle from economics, and basically what it means is that the more you put in to something, the less you get on each extra bit of investment.

e.g. Coke analogy.

So what this means in practice is, I tend to find that takes me about 20% of my time to complete 80% of a project, and completing the last fifth takes 80% of the effort.

And as I get closer and closer to the finish line, eventually I reach a point where the gain is not worth the pain.

Cost-benefit calculations

Whenever you start some work on a project (whether it’s adding a new feature, or browser testing or bug-fixing or whatever), do a quick cost-benefit calculation:

  • How necessary is the work?
  • How many users? (CanIUse / Analytics)
  • How crucial? Will anyone die? Government, medical, Apple etc.
  • What is the opportunity cost?

Know when to let it go

So I think that a big part of being a good developer not just writing good code. It’s also about being flexible, making good cost-benefit calculations, and knowing when it’s not worth your while to push for perfection.
