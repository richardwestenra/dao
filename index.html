2000

But basically, 2000 was the Dark Ages of web development.

It was the the time of the First Browser War.

...Internet Explorer 5 was crushing Netscape 4.5.

...And people were arguing about whether this newfangled CSS thing would take off.

...At the time, there was a bit of a culture war going on.

Print Designers

versus

Web Designers

Most designers then came from a print design background, and they were used to having complete control over how their finished designs would look.

But the web meant new challenges for designers. When you design for the web, you have to take into account that people will be viewing your web page on loads of different computers, and it’ll look completely different from one computer to the next.

You’ve got to consider different screen sizes and resolutions,

different colours, brightness and contrast,

different system fonts and default font-sizes,

and then on top of all that you’ve got different browsers with different rendering engines, each with their own idiosyncratic interpretations of web standards.

alistapart.com/article/dao

And at this point, along comes this bloke from Australia called John Allsop. John Allsop is often regarded as the great-grandad of responsive web design, mainly because he wrote this article in A List Apart called A Dao of Web Design.

He argued that we had to start thinking about design differently, because we’re working with a totally different medium. And he told print designers, “Stop trying to control everything! Let it go!”

The Chaos is a strength

Because when it comes down to it, the flexibility and ubiquity of the web aren’t problems, they’re possibilities.

They make the web more accessible, and mean we can do things we could never have imagined fifteen years ago, in the days of yore.

But that’s ancient history. Let’s come back to 2015. We live in the future!

Perfection
is hard

(So it’s still really hard to make things perfect.)

In fact it’s harder than ever. We have great power, but we still don't have complete control.

Often we end up pushing and pushing for perfection, but we’re up against the law of diminishing returns.

What that means is that beyond a certain point, the more you put into something, the less you get for each extra bit of investment.

And as you get closer and closer to the finish line, eventually you reach a point where the gain is not worth the pain.

So for example, I recently completed a data-vis project that had exactly this problem.

We got it built in just a few days, but then it got stuck in redesign hell for weeks, as we went back and forth over things that we weren’t quite happy with.

The 80/20 rule

This is the 80/20 rule: You can often complete 80% of the project in 20% of the time, but getting from there to the end is a slog.

And sometimes spending five times as long to nail that last 20% is just not worth it.

Effort

versus

Results

So whenever you start some work on a task, take a step back and consider whether it’s worth it.

  • How necessary is the work? Is it worth your time?
  • How many of your users will be affected?
  • How crucial? Will anyone die? Government, medical, etc.
  • What is the opportunity cost?

Let it go

And this applies whether you’re adding a new feature, browser testing, or bug-fixing or even just deciding whether it’s worthwhile taking your time to write some really beautiful code, or whether to just churn out a quick and dirty hack.

Because being a good developer isn’t just writing good code. It’s also about having the knowledge to make good cost-benefit calculations. Sometimes you need push for perfection, but other times, you’ve just gotta let it go.
